</tbody></table><p>The <b>black-winged kite</b> (<i>Elanus caeruleus</i>), also known as the <b>black-shouldered kite</b> (not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Black-shouldered_kite" title="Black-shouldered kite">the closely related Australian species with the same name</a>), is a small diurnal <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a> best known for its habit of hovering over open grasslands in the manner of the much smaller <a href="/wiki/Kestrel" title="Kestrel">kestrels</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Afrotropical" class="mw-redirect" title="Afrotropical">Afrotropical</a> species was sometimes combined with the <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> <a href="/wiki/Black-shouldered_kite" title="Black-shouldered kite">black-shouldered kite</a> (<i>Elanus axillaris</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_kite" title="White-tailed kite">white-tailed kite</a> (<i>Elanus leucurus</i>) of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> which together form a <a href="/wiki/Superspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Superspecies">superspecies</a>. This kite is distinctive, with long wings; white, grey and black plumage; and owl-like forward-facing eyes with red irises. The owl-like behaviour is even more pronounced in the <a href="/wiki/Letter-winged_kite" title="Letter-winged kite">letter-winged kite</a> (<i>Elanus scriptus</i>), a nocturnal relative in Australia. Although mainly seen on plains, they are sometimes seen on grassy slopes of hills in the higher elevation regions of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. They are not migratory, but show nomadism in response to weather and food availability. They are well adapted to utilize periodic upsurges in rodent populations and can raise multiple broods in a single year unlike most birds of prey. Populations in southern Europe have grown in response to human activities, particularly agriculture and livestock rearing.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-winged_kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Elanus_Falco_skulls.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Elanus_Falco_skulls.jpg/220px-Elanus_Falco_skulls.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="178" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Elanus_Falco_skulls.jpg/330px-Elanus_Falco_skulls.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Elanus_Falco_skulls.jpg/440px-Elanus_Falco_skulls.jpg 2x" data-file-width="658" data-file-height="532" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Elanus_Falco_skulls.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Comparison of skull of <i>Elanus</i> with <i>Falco</i> (right)</div></div></div>
<p>The black-winged kite was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> by the French naturalist <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Louiche_Desfontaines" title="René Louiche Desfontaines">René Louiche Desfontaines</a> in 1789 and given the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a> <i>Falco caeruleus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is now one of four species in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i>Elanus</i> which was introduced in 1809 by the French zoologist <a href="/wiki/Marie_Jules_C%C3%A9sar_Savigny" title="Marie Jules César Savigny">Jules-César Savigny</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The genus <i>Elanus</i> is distinctive in having very small scales covering the foot and on the underside, scutellate scales are found only under the terminal phalanges. The claw lacks a groove on the underside.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The word <i> Elanus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language romanization">elanos</i> for a "kite". The specific epithet <i>caeruleus</i> is the Latin for "blue".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are three <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>E. c. caeruleus</i> (<a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Louiche_Desfontaines" title="René Louiche Desfontaines">Desfontaines</a>, 1789) – southwest Iberian Peninsula, Africa, southwest Arabia</li>
<li><i>E. c. vociferus</i> (<a href="/wiki/John_Latham_(ornithologist)" title="John Latham (ornithologist)">Latham</a>, 1790) – Pakistan to east China, Malay Peninsula and Indochina. The underwing secondaries are smoky grey and nearly white in the nominate subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>E. c. hypoleucus</i> <a href="/wiki/John_Gould" title="John Gould">Gould</a>, 1859 – <a href="/wiki/Greater_Sunda_Islands" title="Greater Sunda Islands">Greater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Sunda_Islands" title="Lesser Sunda Islands">Lesser Sunda Islands</a>, the Philippines, <a href="/wiki/Sulawesi" title="Sulawesi">Sulawesi</a> and New Guinea</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-winged_kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Black-winged_(black-shouldered)_kite,_Elanus_caeruleus,_at_Marievale,_Gauteng,_South_Africa_(45979034625).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Black-winged_%28black-shouldered%29_kite%2C_Elanus_caeruleus%2C_at_Marievale%2C_Gauteng%2C_South_Africa_%2845979034625%29.jpg/220px-Black-winged_%28black-shouldered%29_kite%2C_Elanus_caeruleus%2C_at_Marievale%2C_Gauteng%2C_South_Africa_%2845979034625%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Black-winged_%28black-shouldered%29_kite%2C_Elanus_caeruleus%2C_at_Marievale%2C_Gauteng%2C_South_Africa_%2845979034625%29.jpg/330px-Black-winged_%28black-shouldered%29_kite%2C_Elanus_caeruleus%2C_at_Marievale%2C_Gauteng%2C_South_Africa_%2845979034625%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Black-winged_%28black-shouldered%29_kite%2C_Elanus_caeruleus%2C_at_Marievale%2C_Gauteng%2C_South_Africa_%2845979034625%29.jpg/440px-Black-winged_%28black-shouldered%29_kite%2C_Elanus_caeruleus%2C_at_Marievale%2C_Gauteng%2C_South_Africa_%2845979034625%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3382" data-file-height="2258" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Black-winged_(black-shouldered)_kite,_Elanus_caeruleus,_at_Marievale,_Gauteng,_South_Africa_(45979034625).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>E. c. caeruleus</i> hovering with whitish underside of the secondaries visible.</div></div></div>
<p>This long-winged raptor is predominantly grey or white with black shoulder patches, wing tips and eye stripe. The long <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon-like</a> wings extend beyond the tail when the bird is perched. In flight, the short and square tail is visible and it is not forked as in the typical kites of the genus <i>Milvus</i>. When perched, often on roadside wires, it often adjusts its wings and jerks its tail up and down as if to balance itself. The sexes are alike in plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Their large forward-facing eyes placed under a bony shelf that shades them is distinctive; their velvety plumage and zygodactyl feet<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> are characters shared with owls and the genus has been considered as a <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogeny)" class="mw-redirect" title="Basal (phylogeny)">basal</a> group within the Accipitridae.<sup id="cite_ref-Negro2006_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Negro2006-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> They are thought to have been adapted for living in savanna habitats where seasonal rodent population peaks occur. Such food resources are also favoured by the owls. The inner vanes of the feathers have velvety barbules.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> They have a diploid chromosome number of 68 (some older studies claimed 64 for <i>E.c.caeruleus</i> and 66 for <i>E.c.vociferus</i>) with a distinct <a href="/wiki/Karyotype" title="Karyotype">karyotype</a> with resemblances to the kites and honey buzzards and suggesting a basal position within the diurnal birds of prey.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-winged_kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The black-winged kite is a species primarily of open land and semi-deserts in sub-<a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Saharan</a> <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and tropical <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, but it has a foothold within <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> in Spain and <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>. The species range appears to be expanding in southern Europe and possibly in West Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-Balbontin2008_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Balbontin2008-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Salim2002_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salim2002-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sehatisabet2006_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sehatisabet2006-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The first records of breeding in Europe were in the 1860s and since then they have become more widespread and populations are on the rise. It is thought that land-use changes, particularly agriculture and pastureland have helped the species.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Elanus_caeruleus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Elanus_caeruleus.jpg/220px-Elanus_caeruleus.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="229" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Elanus_caeruleus.jpg/330px-Elanus_caeruleus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Elanus_caeruleus.jpg/440px-Elanus_caeruleus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="895" data-file-height="931" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Elanus_caeruleus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Immature <i>E. c. caeruleus</i> from Namibia. The iris is dark in young birds.</div></div></div>
<p>Several geographic populations have been named as subspecies and these include the nominate subspecies which occurs in Spain, Africa and <a href="/wiki/Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabia">Arabia</a>. The subspecies <i>vociferus</i> is found east of this range across South Asia and into Southeast Asia. Along <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a>, <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> subspecies <i>hypoleucus</i> (sometimes considered a full species) includes the population <i>wahgiensis</i> described from New Guinea. Subspecies <i>sumatranus</i> is not always recognized. The <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_kite" title="White-tailed kite">white-tailed kite</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Black-shouldered_kite" title="Black-shouldered kite">black-shouldered kite</a> were formerly included with this species but have since been treated as separate species.<sup id="cite_ref-Clark1992_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clark1992-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hussain1959_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hussain1959-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although found mainly on the plains they have been seen at higher altitudes in <a href="/wiki/Sikkim" title="Sikkim">Sikkim</a> (3,650&#160;m (11,980&#160;ft)),<sup id="cite_ref-Ganguli-Lachungpa1990_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ganguli-Lachungpa1990-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Nilgiri_mountains" class="mw-redirect" title="Nilgiri mountains">Nilgiris</a> (Doddabetta, 2,670&#160;m (8,760&#160;ft))<sup id="cite_ref-Krishna1979_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Krishna1979-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Nagaland" title="Nagaland">Nagaland</a> (2,020&#160;m (6,630&#160;ft)).<sup id="cite_ref-Choudhury1996_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Choudhury1996-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>They are said to be winter visitors in some parts of their range such as the <a href="/wiki/Western_Ghats" title="Western Ghats">Western Ghats</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-winged_kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Black-winged_kite_02.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Black-winged_kite_02.jpg/220px-Black-winged_kite_02.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Black-winged_kite_02.jpg/330px-Black-winged_kite_02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Black-winged_kite_02.jpg/440px-Black-winged_kite_02.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2186" data-file-height="1457" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Black-winged_kite_02.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A courting pair (<i>E. c. vociferus</i>) with a rodent</div></div></div>
<p>The black-winged kite breeds at different times of the year across its range. Although nesting has been noted throughout the year in India, they appear not to breed in April and May. Males establish territories and defend them from competition. Females move into the territories of males. Studies in Africa found that males were more numerous than females.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Courtship is noisy and involves chases and once the pair is formed they copulate frequently.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferrero2003_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferrero2003-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a> is a loose platform of twigs in which 3 or 4 eggs are laid. The female spends more effort in the construction of the nest than the male. The eggs are pale creamy with spots of deep red. Both parents incubate but when the chicks hatch, the male spends more time on foraging for food.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Females initially feed the young, sometimes hunting close to the nest but will also receive food from the male. After <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledging</a> the young birds continue to be dependent for food on the male parent for about 80 days, initially transferring food at perch and later in the air.<sup id="cite_ref-Bustamante1993_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bustamante1993-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Young birds have reddish brown feathers on the upperparts and on the breast. The reddish colour is derived from porphyrins and is thought to provide the young birds some camouflage.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Once breeding is complete females often move on to new territories sometimes deserting before the young fledge, leaving males to feed and raise the young. Both males and females show considerable nomadism.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Unlike most birds of prey, they are capable of raising multiple broods in a year, and young birds are known to disperse widely, adaptations that helps them utilize periodic rodent population surges.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Their opportunistic breeding capabilities are also accompanied by irregular patterns of moult. Young birds show "arrested" moult, retaining feathers for a season and then rapidly moulting them in a serial descendent pattern, where more than one primary feather is moulted at the same time. The adult plumage is found after two years.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Their prey includes grasshoppers, crickets and other large insects, lizards, and rodents. Injured birds, small snakes and frogs have also been reported.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The black-winged kite flies slowly during hunting like a harrier, but it will also hover like a <a href="/wiki/Kestrel_(bird)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kestrel (bird)">Kestrel</a>. It has on rare occasions been known to hunt prey in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-Lamba1969_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lamba1969-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Perches are used for hunting and for feeding but large prey may sometimes be handled on the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-Tarboton1978_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tarboton1978-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In southern Africa, they appear to favour roadside verges for foraging and are sometimes killed by collisions with <a href="/wiki/Roadkill" title="Roadkill">vehicles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dean2003_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dean2003-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Elanus_caeruleus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.94.10.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Elanus_caeruleus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.94.10.jpg/220px-Elanus_caeruleus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.94.10.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="126" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Elanus_caeruleus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.94.10.jpg/330px-Elanus_caeruleus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.94.10.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Elanus_caeruleus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.94.10.jpg/440px-Elanus_caeruleus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.94.10.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4252" data-file-height="2432" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Elanus_caeruleus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.94.10.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs</div></div></div>
<p>These birds roost communally with groups of 15 to 35 (larger numbers in Europe<sup id="cite_ref-Parejo2001_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parejo2001-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>) converging at a large leafy tree.<sup id="cite_ref-Bharos1997_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bharos1997-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> They are extremely silent and the calls recorded include a high-pitched squeal or a soft whistle.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> They call mainly during the breeding season and at the roost site.<sup id="cite_ref-Sharma1993_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sharma1993-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Srinivas2002_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Srinivas2002-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
A species of <a href="/wiki/Nematode" title="Nematode">nematode</a>, <i>Physaloptera acuticauda</i>, has been recorded as a parasite of the species in South Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Ransom1904_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ransom1904-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> A trematode species, <i>Neodiplostomum elani</i>,  has been described from a black-winged kite <a href="/wiki/Type_(biology)" title="Type (biology)">type host</a> from Chandigarh.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> A feather louse <i>Degeeriella elani</i> is known from the species.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The soft feathers of black-winged kites can lead to adherence with some grass seeds which may be dispersed by the species. However such seeds can pose the risk of entangling their wings.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
