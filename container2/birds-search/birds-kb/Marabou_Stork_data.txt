<p>The <b>marabou stork</b> (<i>Leptoptilos crumenifer</i>) is a large wading <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Stork" title="Stork">stork</a> family <a href="/wiki/Ciconiidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Ciconiidae">Ciconiidae</a>. It breeds in Africa south of the Sahara, in both wet and arid habitats, often near human habitation, especially <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfill</a> sites. It is sometimes called the "<a href="/wiki/Funeral_director" title="Funeral director">undertaker</a> bird" due to its shape from behind: cloak-like wings and back, skinny white legs, and sometimes a large white mass of "hair".
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marabou_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name marabou is thought to be derived from the Arabic word <i><a href="/wiki/Marabout" title="Marabout">murābit</a></i> meaning quiet or hermit-like.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The species was originally described in the stork genus <i>Ciconia</i> as <i>Ciconia crumenifera</i> by Lesson. The species was moved into the genus <i>Leptoptilos</i> and the ending was modified to <i>crumeniferus</i> and used by many authors until it was noted that the correct masculine ending to match the genus is <i>crumenifer</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marabou_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The marabou stork is a massive <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a>: large specimens are thought to reach a height of 152&#160;cm (60&#160;in) and a weight of 9&#160;kg (20&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> A wingspan of 3.7&#160;m (12&#160;ft) was accepted by Fisher and Peterson, who ranked the species as having the largest wing-spread of any living bird. Even higher measurements of up to 4.06&#160;m (13.3&#160;ft) have been reported, although no measurement over 3.20&#160;m (10.5&#160;ft) has been verified<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. It is often credited with the largest spread of any landbird, to rival the <a href="/wiki/Andean_condor" title="Andean condor">Andean condor</a>; more typically, however, these storks measure 225–287&#160;cm (7–9&#160;ft) across the wings, which is about a foot less than the average Andean condor wingspan and nearly two feet less than the average of the largest <a href="/wiki/Albatross" title="Albatross">albatrosses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pelican" title="Pelican">pelicans</a>. Typical weight is 4.5–8&#160;kg (9.9–17.6&#160;lb), unusually as low as 4&#160;kg (8.8&#160;lb), and length (from bill to tail) is 120 to 130&#160;cm (47 to 51&#160;in). Females are smaller than males. Bill length can range from 26.4 to 35&#160;cm (10.4 to 13.8&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Unlike most storks, the three <i><a href="/wiki/Leptoptilos" title="Leptoptilos">Leptoptilos</a></i> species fly with the neck retracted like a <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">heron</a>.
</p><p>The marabou is unmistakable due to its size, bare head and neck, black back, and white underparts. It has a huge bill, a pink <a href="/wiki/Gular_sac" class="mw-redirect" title="Gular sac">gular sac</a> at its throat (<i>crumenifer(us)</i> means "carrier of a pouch for money"), a neck ruff, and black legs and wings. The sexes are alike, but the young bird is browner and has a smaller bill. Full maturity is not reached for up to four years.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior_and_ecology">Behavior and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marabou_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behavior and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like most storks, the marabou is gregarious and a <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonial breeder</a>. In the African dry season (when food is more readily available as the pools shrink), it builds a tree nest in which two or three <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> are laid. It is known to be quite ill-tempered.
</p><p>It also resembles other storks in that it is not very vocal, but indulges in bill-rattling <a href="/wiki/Display_(zoology)" title="Display (zoology)">courtship displays</a>. The throat sac is also used to make various noises at that time.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marabou_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The marabou stork breeds in colonies, starting during the dry season. The female lays two to three eggs in a small nest made of sticks; eggs hatch after an incubation period of 30 days. Their young reach sexual maturity at 4 years of age. Lifespan is 41 years in captivity and 25 years in wild.<sup id="cite_ref-adw_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-adw-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marabou_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The marabou stork is a frequent <a href="/wiki/Scavenger" title="Scavenger">scavenger</a>, and the naked head and long neck are adaptations to this livelihood, as it is with the <a href="/wiki/Old_World_vulture" title="Old World vulture">vultures</a> with which the stork often feeds. In both cases, a <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathered</a> head would become rapidly clotted with <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> and other substances when the bird's head was inside a large corpse, and the bare head is easier to keep clean.
</p><p>This large and powerful bird eats mainly <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">carrion</a>, scraps, and <a href="/wiki/Faeces" class="mw-redirect" title="Faeces">faeces</a> but will opportunistically eat almost any animal matter it can swallow. It occasionally eats other birds including <i><a href="/wiki/Quelea" title="Quelea">Quelea</a></i> nestlings, <a href="/wiki/Pigeon" class="mw-redirect" title="Pigeon">pigeons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dove" class="mw-redirect" title="Dove">doves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pelican" title="Pelican">pelican</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cormorant" title="Cormorant">cormorant</a> chicks, and even <a href="/wiki/Flamingo" title="Flamingo">flamingos</a>. During the breeding season, adults scale back on carrion and take mostly small, live prey since nestlings need this kind of food to survive. Common prey at this time may consist of fish, frogs, insects, eggs, small mammals and reptiles such as crocodile hatchlings and eggs,<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and lizards and snakes.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Though known to eat putrid and seemingly inedible foods, these storks may sometimes wash food in water to remove soil.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When feeding on carrion, marabou frequently follow vultures, which are better equipped with hooked bills for tearing through carrion meat and may wait for the vultures to cast aside a piece, steal a piece of meat directly from the vulture or wait until the vultures are done.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> As with vultures, marabou storks perform an important natural function by cleaning areas via their ingestion of carrion and waste.
</p><p>Increasingly, marabous have become dependent on human garbage and hundreds of the huge birds can be found around African dumps or waiting for a hand out in urban areas. Marabous eating human garbage have been seen to devour virtually anything that they can swallow, including shoes and pieces of metal. Marabous conditioned to eating from human sources have been known to lash out when refused food.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats">Threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marabou_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A number of <a href="/wiki/Endoparasites" class="mw-redirect" title="Endoparasites">endoparasites</a> have been identified in wild marabous including <i>Cheilospirura</i>, <i>Echinura</i> and <i>Acuaria</i> <a href="/wiki/Nematodes" class="mw-redirect" title="Nematodes">nematodes</a>, <i>Amoebotaenia sphenoides</i> (Cestoda) and <i>Dicrocoelium hospes</i> (Trematoda).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_uses">Human uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marabou_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Human uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Marabou_(fashion)" title="Marabou (fashion)">Marabou (fashion)</a></div>
<p>Marabou down is frequently used in the trimming of various items of clothing and hats, as well as <a href="/wiki/Fishing_lures" class="mw-redirect" title="Fishing lures">fishing lures</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Turkey down and similar feathers have been used as a substitute for making 'marabou' trimming.<sup id="cite_ref-terry_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-terry-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
