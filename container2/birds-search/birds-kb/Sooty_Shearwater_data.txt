<p>The <b>sooty shearwater</b> (<i>Ardenna grisea</i>) is a medium-large <a href="/wiki/Shearwater" title="Shearwater">shearwater</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> family <a href="/wiki/Procellariidae" title="Procellariidae">Procellariidae</a>. <i>Ardenna</i> was first used to refer to a seabird by Italian naturalist <a href="/wiki/Ulisse_Aldrovandi" title="Ulisse Aldrovandi">Ulisse Aldrovandi</a> in 1603, and <i>grisea</i> is medieval <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "grey".<sup id="cite_ref-job90_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job90-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In New Zealand, it is also known by its <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language" title="Māori language">Māori</a> name <i lang="mi" title="Māori language text">tītī</i>, and as <a href="/wiki/Muttonbird_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Muttonbird (disambiguation)">muttonbird</a>, like its relatives the <a href="/wiki/Wedge-tailed_shearwater" title="Wedge-tailed shearwater">wedge-tailed shearwater</a> (<i>A. pacificus</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> <a href="/wiki/Short-tailed_shearwater" title="Short-tailed shearwater">short-tailed shearwater</a> (<i>A. tenuirostris</i>).
</p><p>It appears to be particularly closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Great_shearwater" title="Great shearwater">great shearwater</a> (<i>A. gravis</i>) and the short-tailed shearwater, all blunt-tailed, black-billed species, but its precise relationships are obscure.<sup id="cite_ref-Austin1996_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Austin1996-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Heidrich_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heidrich-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Austin2004_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Austin2004-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In any case, these three species are among the larger species of shearwaters that have been moved into a separate genus <i>Ardenna</i> based on a phylogenetic analysis of <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Penhallurick_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Penhallurick-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rheindt_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rheindt-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sooty_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sooty_shearwater_kaikoura.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Sooty_shearwater_kaikoura.jpg/220px-Sooty_shearwater_kaikoura.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Sooty_shearwater_kaikoura.jpg/330px-Sooty_shearwater_kaikoura.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Sooty_shearwater_kaikoura.jpg/440px-Sooty_shearwater_kaikoura.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2028" data-file-height="1348" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sooty_shearwater_kaikoura.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Up close, the chocolate-coloured <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> can be appreciated.</div></div></div>
<p>Sooty shearwaters are 40–51&#160;cm in length with a 94– to 110-cm wingspan.<sup id="cite_ref-McGonigal_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGonigal-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> It has the typical "shearing" flight of the genus, dipping from side to side on stiff wings with few wing beats, the wingtips almost touching the water. Its flight is powerful and direct, with wings held stiff and straight, giving the impression of a very small <a href="/wiki/Albatross" title="Albatross">albatross</a>. This shearwater is identifiable by its dark <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>, which is responsible for its name. In poor viewing conditions, it looks all black, but in good light, it shows as dark chocolate-brown with a silvery strip along the center of the underwing.
</p><p>Usually loud, sooty shearwaters coo and croak while on the breeding grounds.
</p><p>In the Atlantic, it is the only such bird, whereas in the Pacific part of its range, other all-dark large shearwaters are found. The short-tailed shearwater in particular is almost impossible to tell apart from the present species at a distance.<sup id="cite_ref-wanderwerf2006_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wanderwerf2006-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_movements">Distribution and movements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sooty_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and movements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Puffinus_griseus_-near_Avila_Beach,_California,_USA_-upper_body-8a.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Puffinus_griseus_-near_Avila_Beach%2C_California%2C_USA_-upper_body-8a.jpg/220px-Puffinus_griseus_-near_Avila_Beach%2C_California%2C_USA_-upper_body-8a.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Puffinus_griseus_-near_Avila_Beach%2C_California%2C_USA_-upper_body-8a.jpg/330px-Puffinus_griseus_-near_Avila_Beach%2C_California%2C_USA_-upper_body-8a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Puffinus_griseus_-near_Avila_Beach%2C_California%2C_USA_-upper_body-8a.jpg/440px-Puffinus_griseus_-near_Avila_Beach%2C_California%2C_USA_-upper_body-8a.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1050" data-file-height="700" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Puffinus_griseus_-near_Avila_Beach,_California,_USA_-upper_body-8a.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Upper body of a bird swimming off the shore of California</div></div></div>
<p>Sooty shearwaters breed on small islands in the south <a href="/wiki/Pacific" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific">Pacific</a> and south <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> Oceans, mainly around <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tierra_del_Fuego" title="Tierra del Fuego">Tierra del Fuego</a>, and in the <a href="/wiki/Auckland_Islands" title="Auckland Islands">Auckland Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phillip_Island_(Norfolk_Island)" title="Phillip Island (Norfolk Island)">Phillip Island off Norfolk Island</a>. They start breeding in October, and incubate their young for about 54 days. Once the chick hatches, the parents raise their chick for 86 to 109 days.<sup id="cite_ref-McGonigal_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGonigal-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>They are spectacular long-distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrants</a>, following a circular route,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> traveling north up the western side of the Pacific and Atlantic Oceans at the end of the nesting season in March–May, reaching subarctic waters in June–July, where they cross from west to east, then return south down the eastern side of the oceans in September–October, reaching to the breeding colonies in November. They do not migrate as a flock, but rather as individuals, associating only opportunistically; in June 1906, for example, two were shot near <a href="/wiki/Guadalupe_Island" title="Guadalupe Island">Guadalupe Island</a> off <a href="/wiki/Baja_California" title="Baja California">Baja California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, several weeks before the bulk of the population would pass by.<sup id="cite_ref-Thayer_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thayer-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Likewise, the identity of numerous large, dark shearwaters observed in October 2004 off <a href="/wiki/Kwajalein" class="mw-redirect" title="Kwajalein">Kwajalein</a> in the Marshall Islands remain enigmatic; they might have been either sooty or short-tailed shearwaters, but neither species is generally held to pass through this region at that time.<sup id="cite_ref-wanderwerf2006_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wanderwerf2006-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the Atlantic Ocean, they cover distances in excess of 14,000&#160;km (8,700&#160;mi) from their breeding colony on the Falkland Islands (52°S 60°W) north to 60 to 70°N in the North Atlantic Ocean off north <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>; distances covered in the Pacific are similar or larger; although the Pacific Ocean colonies are not quite so far south, at 35 to 50°S off New Zealand, and moving north to the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Islands" title="Aleutian Islands">Aleutian Islands</a>, the longitudinal width of the ocean makes longer migrations necessary. Recent tagging experiments have shown that birds breeding in New Zealand may travel 74,000&#160;km in a year, reaching Japan, Alaska, and California, averaging more than 500&#160;km per day.<sup id="cite_ref-shafferetal2006_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shafferetal2006-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, they move south in late August and September; with strong north and north-west winds, they may occasionally become "trapped" in the shallow, largely enclosed <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>, and heavy passages<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Does &#39;heavy passages&#39; mean &#39;large groups&#39;? (June 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> may be seen flying back north up the British east coast as they retrace their path back to the Atlantic over northern <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology_and_status">Ecology and status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sooty_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology and status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Puffinus_griseus_-near_Avila_Beach,_California,_USA_-many-8a_(2).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Puffinus_griseus_-near_Avila_Beach%2C_California%2C_USA_-many-8a_%282%29.jpg/220px-Puffinus_griseus_-near_Avila_Beach%2C_California%2C_USA_-many-8a_%282%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Puffinus_griseus_-near_Avila_Beach%2C_California%2C_USA_-many-8a_%282%29.jpg/330px-Puffinus_griseus_-near_Avila_Beach%2C_California%2C_USA_-many-8a_%282%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Puffinus_griseus_-near_Avila_Beach%2C_California%2C_USA_-many-8a_%282%29.jpg/440px-Puffinus_griseus_-near_Avila_Beach%2C_California%2C_USA_-many-8a_%282%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1040" data-file-height="750" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Puffinus_griseus_-near_Avila_Beach,_California,_USA_-many-8a_(2).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A small portion of a huge flock off the shore of California, United States in September</div></div></div>
<p>The sooty shearwater feeds on <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a>. They can dive up to 68&#160;m deep for food,<sup id="cite_ref-shafferetal2006_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shafferetal2006-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> but more commonly take surface food, in particular often following <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">whales</a> to catch fish disturbed by them. They also follow fishing boats to take fish scraps thrown overboard.
</p><p>They breed in huge colonies and the female lays one white egg. These shearwaters nest in <a href="/wiki/Burrow_nest" class="mw-redirect" title="Burrow nest">burrows</a> lined with plant material, which are visited only at night to avoid predation by large <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>. The architecture of sooty shearwater burrows can vary within and between breeding colonies, and is influenced by competition for breeding space and habitat type, with soil under dense <a href="/wiki/Tussac_grass" class="mw-redirect" title="Tussac grass">tussac grass</a> being easier to excavate than other substrates.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In New Zealand, about 250,000 muttonbirds are <a href="/wiki/Muttonbirding" title="Muttonbirding">harvested</a> for oils and food each year by the indigenous Māori population.<sup id="cite_ref-McGonigal_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGonigal-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Young birds just about to fledge are collected from the burrows, plucked, and often preserved in salt.
</p><p>Its numbers have been declining in recent decades, and it is presently classified as <a href="/wiki/Near_threatened" class="mw-redirect" title="Near threatened">near threatened</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In 2009, the harvest reported record-low catches, on average a trapping cage yielded nearly 500 birds; in 2009, the number was estimated to be closer to 40 per cage.
</p>
<h2><span id="Inspiration_for_Hitchcock.27s_The_Birds"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Inspiration_for_Hitchcock's_The_Birds">Inspiration for Hitchcock's <i>The Birds</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sooty_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Inspiration for Hitchcock&#039;s The Birds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On August 18, 1961, the <i><a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz_Sentinel" title="Santa Cruz Sentinel">Santa Cruz Sentinel</a></i> reported that thousands of crazed sooty shearwaters<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> were sighted on the shores of North <a href="/wiki/Monterey_Bay" title="Monterey Bay">Monterey Bay</a> in California, regurgitating anchovies, flying into objects, and dying on the streets. The incident sparked the interest of local resident <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">Alfred Hitchcock</a>, along with a story about spooky bird behavior by British writer <a href="/wiki/Daphne_du_Maurier" title="Daphne du Maurier">Daphne du Maurier</a>, helping to inspire Hitchcock's 1963 thriller <i><a href="/wiki/The_Birds_(film)" title="The Birds (film)">The Birds</a></i>, a cautionary tale of nature revolting against man.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The film is now ranked among the <a href="/wiki/American_Film_Institute" title="American Film Institute">American Film Institute</a>'s top-10 thrillers of the last century.
</p><p>Scientists looking at the stomach contents of turtles and seabirds gathered in 1961 Monterey Bay ship surveys have found toxin-making algae were present in 79% of the plankton<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> the creatures ate. "I am pretty convinced that the birds were poisoned," says ocean environmentalist Sibel Bargu of Louisiana State University. "All the symptoms were extremely similar to later bird poisoning events in the same area."
</p><p>Plankton expert Raphael Kudela of USC points to leaky septic tanks installed amid a housing boom around Monterey Bay in the early 1960s as the ultimate culprit that may have fed the toxic algae<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> "It is to some extent a natural phenomenon, and the best thing we can do is monitor for the presence of toxins, and treat impacted wildlife."
</p>
